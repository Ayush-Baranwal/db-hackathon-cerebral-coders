What is dementia?

Dementia is a general term for a decline in cognitive function severe enough to interfere with daily life. It affects memory, thinking, orientation, comprehension, calculation, learning capacity, language, and judgment.

What are the common types of dementia?

The most common types of dementia include Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, and frontotemporal dementia.

What are the early signs and symptoms of dementia?

Early signs include memory loss, difficulty with problem-solving, confusion about time or place, trouble understanding visual information, and changes in mood or personality.

How is dementia diagnosed?

Diagnosis typically involves a combination of medical history, physical examinations, neurological tests, mental status assessments, and brain imaging (such as MRI or CT scans).

Can dementia be prevented?

While there is no guaranteed way to prevent dementia, maintaining a healthy lifestyle with regular exercise, a balanced diet, mental stimulation, and social engagement may reduce the risk.

What treatment options are available for dementia?

Treatments include medications to manage symptoms, cognitive therapies, occupational therapy, and lifestyle changes to support brain health. Supportive care and counseling can also be beneficial.

How can I support a loved one with dementia?

Support includes providing a safe and structured environment, maintaining a routine, offering emotional support, encouraging social activities, and seeking professional help when needed.

What are the stages of dementia?

Dementia generally progresses through three stages: mild (early stage), moderate (middle stage), and severe (late stage), with symptoms worsening over time.

Where can I find resources and support for dementia?

Resources are available through healthcare providers, local support groups, national organizations like the Alzheimer's Association, and online platforms that offer information, support networks, and caregiving advice.
